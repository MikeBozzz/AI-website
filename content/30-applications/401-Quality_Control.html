Quality Control using AI

Michelangelo Bettini • 12.11.2022

For many manufacturers, the real cost of production quality can be more than 15% of sales revenue.1 A quality control done by a human is subject to flaws, determined by a lot of variables, which leads to loss of money and costumer dissatisfaction. That's why an AI based quality control is overtaking the factories. The softwares developed most of the time are universal, therefore it can be applied to a variety of products that are produced inside the same facility. The instantaneity that the AI can give also guarantees that the production process is not slowed down.

A leader in this sector is Google's Visual Inspection AI.2 It can execute an inspection on a variety of cases, such as:

  • Manufacturing inspection tasks

  • Welding seam inspection

  • Mobile phone PCB inspection

  • Silicon wafer defect analysis

So let's see an example case for a deployment of this AI:

Let's say that a semiconductor fab in Taiwan is looking to improve their silicon wafer and semiconductor chip yield by detecting defects at the source and reducing waste to meet their increased production targets. A production field engineer is taking the first steps to automate the defect detection with the visual inspection AI solution.

  1. He first collects images of the wafers and imports them into a visual inspection AI data set.

  2. He then creates labels for the type of defects such as scratches and discoloration. The simple user interface helps him quickly annotate defect areas on the images.

  3. Unlike other solutions, he only needs to annotate 10 images for each defect type. That's enough for the visual inspection AI model to start his training.

  4. After the training is done, the production field engineer reviews the model's performance against key metrics like precision and recall.

  5. Once deployed, the model can run either autonomously on premises or in the cloud. It can also be deployed at scale across multiple fabs.

For most fabrication plants, the normal avoidable cost of production quality issues can easily reach $55 million per year.1
